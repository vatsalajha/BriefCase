I. INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff DataVault Inc. ("DataVault") respectfully submits this Memorandum of
Law in support of its Motion for Summary Judgment against Defendant Pacific
Technologies, LLC ("Pacific Tech"). This action arises from Pacific Tech's
deliberate and systematic breach of a Software Integration Agreement ("the
Agreement"), the unlawful misappropriation of DataVault's proprietary trade
secrets, and Pacific Tech's flagrant violation of federal and state data privacy
laws through the unauthorized access, extraction, and commercial exploitation of
personally identifiable information belonging to over 2.3 million DataVault
platform users.

The undisputed material facts demonstrate that Pacific Tech: (1) breached
express contractual obligations governing the handling and security of user data;
(2) misappropriated DataVault's proprietary algorithms, customer segmentation
models, and database schemas constituting protectable trade secrets under the
Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836 et seq. ("DTSA"); and (3) violated
applicable data privacy regulations, including the California Consumer Privacy
Act, Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq. ("CCPA"), as amended by the California
Privacy Rights Act ("CPRA"), and applicable Federal Trade Commission enforcement
guidelines governing unfair or deceptive data practices. No genuine issue of
material fact exists, and DataVault is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

A. The Parties and Their Business Relationship

DataVault Inc. is a leading cloud-based data management and analytics platform
incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in San Francisco,
California. DataVault provides enterprise SaaS solutions to over 4,000 corporate
clients across seventeen industries, processing sensitive customer data pursuant
to rigorous security protocols certified under SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001.

Pacific Technologies, LLC is a California software development firm that entered
into the Software Integration Agreement with DataVault on March 15, 2022. Under
the Agreement, Pacific Tech was granted a limited, revocable license to access
DataVault's Application Programming Interface ("API") for the sole purpose of
developing a complementary data visualization module. In exchange, Pacific Tech
agreed to handle all accessed data in strict compliance with DataVault's Data
Processing Addendum ("DPA"), the CCPA/CPRA, and applicable FTC data security
guidelines.

B. The Non-Compete and Confidentiality Provisions

Section 12.4 of the Agreement contains the following non-compete covenant:

    "Employee and Contractor personnel agrees that during the term of this
    Agreement and for a period of twenty-four (24) months following the
    termination of engagement for any reason, neither party shall, directly
    or indirectly, engage in, own, manage, operate, control, be employed by,
    participate in, or be connected with any business that competes with the
    Company's core data management and analytics business within a fifty (50)
    mile radius of any office location where the engaged personnel was
    assigned during the last two (2) years of the engagement."

Section 12.6 of the Agreement contains the following arbitration clause:

    "All disputes arising out of or relating to this Agreement, including
    any question regarding its existence, validity or termination, shall be
    referred to and finally resolved by binding arbitration administered by
    the American Arbitration Association in accordance with its Commercial
    Arbitration Rules. The seat of arbitration shall be San Francisco,
    California. The language of the arbitration shall be English. There
    shall be a single arbitrator."

These provisions were negotiated at arm's length between sophisticated commercial
parties each represented by experienced counsel.

C. Pacific Tech's Unlawful Conduct

Beginning in approximately September 2023, DataVault's security monitoring
systems detected anomalous API access patterns attributable to Pacific Tech's
integration credentials. Forensic analysis subsequently revealed that Pacific
Tech had, without authorization, systematically extracted over 847 gigabytes of
raw user data — including names, email addresses, behavioral analytics, and
proprietary segmentation cluster assignments — through a series of automated
scraping scripts disguised as routine API calls.

DataVault's forensic investigators further determined that Pacific Tech had
copied DataVault's proprietary recommendation engine source code, customer
lifetime value prediction models, and database schema architecture — all of
which constitute trade secrets developed by DataVault over eight years at a cost
exceeding $23 million in research and development expenditure.

Upon discovery, DataVault immediately terminated the Agreement, notified affected
users in compliance with its CCPA/CPRA obligations, and reported the incident to
the California Attorney General and the Federal Trade Commission. The FTC has
since opened an investigative inquiry into Pacific Tech's data practices.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

Summary judgment is appropriate where "there is no genuine dispute as to any
material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law." Fed. R.
Civ. P. 56(a). A fact is material only if, under the applicable substantive law,
it might affect the outcome of the case. Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477
U.S. 242, 248 (1986). The moving party bears the initial burden of establishing
the absence of a genuine issue of material fact. Celotex Corp. v. Catrett, 477
U.S. 317, 323 (1986). Once satisfied, the burden shifts to the nonmoving party
to present specific facts demonstrating a genuine issue for trial.

IV. ARGUMENT

A. Defendant's Conduct Violates Federal Data Privacy Protections

The Supreme Court has made clear that individuals retain constitutionally
protected privacy interests in their digital data and the records of their
online activities. As the Court held in Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S.
296 (2018), the government — and by extension, private actors operating under
government-delegated data access frameworks — generally must obtain affirmative
consent or lawful process before accessing individuals' digital location
and behavioral information. The Court emphasized that even voluntary disclosure
of data to a third-party service provider does not eliminate a person's
reasonable expectation of privacy in the aggregate picture that data reveals.
Id. at 320. This principle applies with full force here, where Pacific Tech
accessed DataVault users' comprehensive behavioral profiles far beyond the
scope of any consent those users provided.

Pacific Tech's data practices also violate longstanding privacy frameworks
established under federal common law. The foundational privacy doctrine
established in Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113 (1973), recognized that the Due
Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment encompasses a right to privacy that
extends to deeply personal decisions and information — a principle that courts
have consistently applied to limit unauthorized commercial exploitation of
individuals' personal data. While Pacific Tech may argue that commercial data
processing falls outside this framework, courts in this district have uniformly
rejected such cramped readings of constitutional privacy protections.

Moreover, the constitutional right to privacy in one's personal data and digital
associations has been further cemented by the Supreme Court's decision in
Citizens United v. FEC, 558 U.S. 310 (2010), which established that individuals
possess an inherent constitutional right to maintain privacy in their personal
data and associations, free from unauthorized government or commercial
surveillance. Pacific Tech's systematic extraction of user data therefore
constitutes a per se violation of these constitutionally protected rights.

Pacific Tech's claim that it had implicit authorization to access this data is
further refuted by the Ninth Circuit's holding in Henderson v. TechCorp
Solutions, 847 F.3d 1092 (9th Cir. 2019), which held that entities acting as
data controllers owe fiduciary duties to the users whose personal information
they collect, store, and process, and that any secondary commercial use of such
data without explicit consent constitutes a breach of those fiduciary duties
actionable under both federal and state law. Pacific Tech plainly violated these
duties here.

The CCPA/CPRA independently obligates data processors to maintain robust
security controls and prohibits the sale or sharing of consumer personal
information without opt-in consent for sensitive categories of data. Cal. Civ.
Code §§ 1798.100, 1798.121. Pacific Tech's bulk extraction and apparent
commercial resale of DataVault's user data violates each of these provisions.
The FTC has signaled in recent enforcement guidance and proposed rulemaking
under its commercial surveillance authority that precisely this type of covert
data harvesting constitutes an unfair or deceptive practice under Section 5 of
the FTC Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45.

B. The Non-Compete and Confidentiality Provisions Are Enforceable

The contractual protections DataVault negotiated are entitled to full
enforcement. Pacific Tech, as a sophisticated commercial entity represented by
counsel, voluntarily accepted the non-compete covenant and arbitration clause
as part of an arms-length commercial negotiation. The Equal Protection
principles articulated in Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954),
establish that no party is exempt from neutral, generally applicable contract
law simply by virtue of its commercial size or market position — the law applies
equally to all contracting parties.

California courts have repeatedly enforced commercial non-compete covenants
between business entities (as distinct from employee-employer agreements) where,
as here, the restriction is reasonable in scope, duration, and geographic
coverage, and is supported by adequate consideration in the form of access to
proprietary systems, data, and trade secrets. The 24-month, 50-mile radius
restriction at issue here is narrowly tailored to protect DataVault's legitimate
commercial interests and does not constitute an undue burden on Pacific Tech's
general ability to conduct business.

C. Defendant Is Liable for Misappropriation of Trade Secrets Under the DTSA

The Defend Trade Secrets Act provides a federal civil remedy for the
misappropriation of trade secrets used in or intended for use in interstate or
foreign commerce. 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b). DataVault's recommendation engine,
customer lifetime value models, and database schemas clearly qualify as trade
secrets: they derive independent economic value from not being generally known
or readily ascertainable, and DataVault has taken reasonable measures to
maintain their secrecy through technical access controls, contractual
confidentiality obligations, and security certifications.

Pacific Tech's unauthorized copying of this proprietary technology constitutes
misappropriation by both improper acquisition and disclosure within the meaning
of 18 U.S.C. § 1839(5)-(6). Notably, even before custodial interrogation of
its own data security practices begins, Pacific Tech was obligated under the
procedural safeguards established in Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U.S. 436 (1966),
to understand that DataVault's express notice of confidentiality obligations in
the DPA placed Pacific Tech on clear notice that any unauthorized use of the
licensed data or associated trade secrets would subject it to legal liability.
This principle — that a party receiving clear and unambiguous notice of
confidentiality obligations is bound by those obligations — has been uniformly
applied by courts in trade secret and contract cases.

DataVault is entitled to recover actual damages, unjust enrichment, exemplary
damages of up to two times actual damages for willful and malicious
misappropriation, and attorney's fees. 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(3).
